Introduction

Trajectory tracking is an important problem in robotic control and has been widely applied in both industrial and civilian domains. Some examples include high-precision robotic manipulators and unmanned aerial vehicles (e.g., quadrotors) for search and rescue. In this project, we reproduce the study in reference [1], which investigates a particular setting of this problem, referred to as impromptu trajectory tracking.

The core idea of [1] is to use a DNN as an add-on module placed before a stabilized baseline control system to improve impromptu trajectory tracking. In accordance with the proposal and practical constraints, the project mainly consists of three parts.
1) Theory. Since the theoretical part accounts for roughly 35% of paper [1], the theoretical sections of the paper will be analyzed in detail. The more difficult arguments will be further derived and clarified in this final report.
2) Reproduction. Section 5 of [1] was fully reproduced in simulation.
3) Simulation-based exploration. The project will demonstrate the generalization capability of the proposed method and exploring its potential boundaries.

Results

In [1], the baseline system is modeled as a multi-input–multi-output (MIMO) system, and Eq. (1) is a linear time-invariant model. The state space model is:

Linear Time-Invariant Model:
x(k+1) = A ⋅ x(k) + B ⋅ u(k)
y(k) = C ⋅ x(k)

Here, the nonnegative integer k is the discrete-time index, x ∈ R^n is the system state, u ∈ R^m is the reference signal (control), and y ∈ R^m is the system output. A, B, and C are constant. The explicit form of Eq. (1) is shown below, which is more convenient for coding:

Explicit Dimensions:
xn×1(k+1) = An×n ⋅ xn×1(k) + Bn×m ⋅ um×1(k)
ym×1(k) = Cm×n ⋅ xn×1(k)

The nonlinear version of Eq. (1) is given in Eq. (2). Its state-space model is:

Nonlinear Model:
x(k+1) = f(x(k)) + g(x(k)) ⋅ u(k)
y(k) = h(x(k))

Assumptions

  • (A1) Stable: Bounded inputs lead to bounded states.
  • (A2) Future desired trajectory: The future desired trajectory is available.
  • (A3) DNN Smoothness: The DNN has smooth output with respect to inputs (globally Lipschitz).

Definitions

  • Vector Relative Degree (VRR): The number of steps before some input u affects some output y.
  • Full-Rank Decoupling Matrix: Outputs respond independently enough to solve for the control input.

Simulation

We reproduce the simulation presented in Section 5 of [1] to illustrate Insights 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3. Two linear MIMO baseline closed-loop systems are considered, with the same state equation as below.

\[ x(k+1)= \begin{bmatrix} 0.2 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0.6 \end{bmatrix}x(k) + \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix}u(k) \]

It is not hard to see that the matrix \(A\) of the baseline system is upper triangular, so its eigenvalues are its diagonal entries. Moreover, since all these eigenvalues lie inside the unit circle \((<1)\), they correspond to three stable poles. The output equations of the two systems are defined in Eq. (29) and (30), respectively.

\[ y(k)= \begin{bmatrix} 0.35 & 0.35 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix}x(k) \tag{29} \]
\[ y(k)= \begin{bmatrix} -0.35 & 0.35 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0.5 \end{bmatrix}x(k) \tag{30} \]

Conclusion

In this project, we reproduced and analyzed the DNN add-on framework proposed in [1] for impromptu trajectory tracking. The theoretical part was reviewed in detail. The simulation results in Section 5 were also successfully reproduced. Further simulation-based exploration confirms the good generalization ability of the DNN add-on module. The robustness under extreme conditions was discussed and remained an open question. Overall, this project supports the main conclusion of [1]: a theory-guided DNN add-on design can improve tracking performance effectively while keeping the framework relatively simple and economical.
